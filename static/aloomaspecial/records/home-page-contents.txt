Why Do Air Conditioner Symptoms Point in the Wrong Direction?<h2>
An air conditioner that won't cool the house can be a refrigerant issue, restricted airflow, a failing capacitor, a dirty condenser coil, or a thermostat miscommunication. An air conditioner that won't turn on at all can be a tripped breaker, a burned-out contactor, a safety float switch responding to condensate overflow, or a failed capacitor preventing the compressor from starting. The symptom points toward a category. It does not identify the cause.
This is why the diagnostic sequence matters. A technician who goes straight to refrigerant when a system isn't cooling will miss the cases where refrigerant charge is fine but airflow restriction is the problem. That same technician will miss the cases where a fouled condenser coil is driving head pressure high enough to trigger the compressor's thermal protection. An incorrect diagnosis leads to a repair that doesn't hold and a second visit that should have been the first one.

What is delta-T and why does it matter in air conditioning diagnosis? Delta-T is the temperature difference between the air entering your return vents and the conditioned air leaving your supply vents after passing through the evaporator coil. In a properly functioning system, that differential should fall between 16 and 22 degrees Fahrenheit. When it drops below that range, the system is not extracting heat from the air at the designed rate, which points toward a refrigerant, coil, or airflow issue. When it runs above that range, something is restricting airflow across the coil. Measuring delta-T is one of the first steps in any diagnostic because it tells us immediately whether the system is operating in its correct performance window.
