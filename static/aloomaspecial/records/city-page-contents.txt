Why Is Your Air Conditioner Running All Day Without Cooling Your #State Home?<h2>
When an air conditioner runs without reaching the set temperature in a #State home, there are several distinct causes, and identifying the right one requires measurement, not assumption.
The most common cause is a refrigerant charge deficit. When refrigerant drops below the system's specified level, the evaporator coil gets colder than its design temperature. In some cases it ices over. The system's ability to transfer heat from the indoor air into the refrigerant drops significantly. The house feels slightly cooler than outdoors but never reaches the target temperature.
The second common cause is restricted airflow. When the volume of air moving through the system is insufficient — from a clogged filter, a partially closed return grille, undersized ductwork, or a failing blower motor — the evaporator coil cannot process enough air to overcome the home's heat load. The system runs hard and delivers conditioned air, but not enough of it.
A third, less frequently diagnosed cause is an actual mismatch between the system's capacity and the home's current heat load. Homes that have had significant changes in sun exposure, insulation quality, or square footage since the system was installed can reach a point where the equipment is operating correctly at full capacity and simply cannot keep up. Identifying that requires measuring the system's actual output, not estimating it.

A proper performance diagnostic for a #State home that won't cool starts with delta-T measurement: the temperature difference between the air entering the return vents and the air leaving the supply vents after passing through the evaporator coil. AC Condensate Drain Failure in #State: What It Is and Why It Shuts Your System Down<h2>
Every central air conditioning system generates water as part of the cooling process. When warm, humid indoor air passes over the cold evaporator coil, moisture condenses on the coil surface the same way condensation forms on a cold glass. In #State's humid months, a residential AC system can pull one to three gallons of water from the air per hour of operation. That water collects in a drain pan beneath the coil and flows out through a condensate drain line to a floor drain, utility sink, or exterior discharge point.
When the drain line clogs, the water has nowhere to go. The drain pan fills. Most modern #State air conditioning systems include a float switch mounted in the primary drain pan: a small sensor that detects rising water levels. When the water reaches the float switch's trigger point, it cuts power to the system entirely. This is a safety mechanism, not a malfunction. The AC is not broken. It is protecting your home from water damage.
The shutdown is the system working correctly. The problem is the clog that caused it.

How Air Conditioning Systems Degrade Without Annual Service in #State<h2>
An air conditioning system does not fail suddenly because it decided to. It fails because something small was left unaddressed until it became something larger. Understanding the degradation sequence is what turns a tune-up from a service you schedule into a cost you prevent.
Capacitors degrade gradually. These are the electrical components that deliver the high-current startup charge to the compressor motor and fan motor each time the system cycles on. Capacitors are rated in microfarads, and their actual measured value drifts downward over time as heat cycles and electrical stress accumulate. A capacitor rated at 45 microfarads may measure 36 microfarads after four seasons of #State summer use. At that level, the compressor labors to start each cycle, drawing higher current and stressing the motor windings. Failure arrives without visible warning.
Condenser coils collect debris continuously. In #State, coils exposed to trees, landscaping, and seasonal airborne particulates develop a matted layer between the aluminum fins. That layer acts as insulation, trapping heat that should be released to the outdoor air. As the coil's ability to shed heat decreases, the refrigerant pressure in the high side of the system rises. The compressor works against greater resistance on every cycle. Energy consumption goes up. Cooling output goes down. The compressor's lifespan shortens.

HVAC Wiring and Communication: How Thermostats Control Air Conditioning Systems in #State<h2>
Residential HVAC systems in #State communicate between the thermostat and the air handler through a low-voltage wiring system using labeled control terminals. The R terminal carries 24-volt power from the transformer. The C terminal provides a return path for that power. The Y terminal sends the cooling signal to the compressor. The G terminal runs the indoor fan. On two-stage systems, a Y2 signal triggers second-stage compressor capacity.
When any of these connections corrode, loosen, or fail, the behavior that results depends on which terminal is affected. A failed C wire causes smart thermostats to drain their battery rapidly and restart unexpectedly. A failed Y wire means the compressor never receives the cooling signal, so the fan runs and air circulates but no cooling happens. A failed R connection means no signals reach any component at all.
Diagnosing a wiring fault requires checking voltage at each terminal under operating conditions, not just verifying the wires are physically present. A wire can be seated in a terminal and still carry no signal if the connection is corroded or the strip length is too short to make solid contact. In #State's climate, where humidity accelerates terminal corrosion in older systems, this is a routine finding.
